How I Work

Research that goes past the obvious.

Discovery research on complex systems means going to where the work actually happens — not designing for an assumed user on an assumed day.

🔍

Field-First Discovery

I run contextual inquiry and observational sessions on-site with real users doing real work. Surveys and remote interviews alone do not surface the workarounds, informal authority structures, and tacit knowledge that define how complex systems actually function day to day.

🗺

Systems-Level Synthesis

I produce service blueprints and multi-actor process flows that trace work across users, back-office staff, systems, and handoff points — documenting what is happening in full, not just the user-facing layer, so organizations can see where the real problems sit.

📋

Validated, Actionable Findings

Every deliverable is validated with participants before it reaches leadership. Research reports, personas, and requirements are structured around the decisions the executive and technical audience needs to make — not around research process for its own sake.

Healthcare Multi-Stakeholder
Public Hospital System — [Redacted] · 2023

Referral & Intake Permitting Workflow — Discovery Research

Ran discovery research across six user groups inside a regional public hospital system, conducting contextual inquiry and observation sessions across three facilities. Documented thirty-two system touchpoints and eleven staff-built workarounds in a cross-actor service blueprint used by agency leadership to plan the platform redesign.

Service Blueprint Persona Set (6) Process Flow Executive Briefing UX Requirements

Background

The hospital system had a documented referral and intake workflow — a six-step process on paper. In practice, staff across three facilities had built parallel spreadsheets, phone-based reconciliation loops, and informal approval chains to compensate for gaps no one had ever formally mapped. The agency commissioned discovery research before any redesign work began.

"We knew there were problems. We did not know where they actually lived, who was affected, or what was causing them. That was the work."

Research Methods

Contextual InquiryField Observation Stakeholder InterviewsArtifact Analysis Co-Design WorkshopsParticipant Validation

Each on-site session was structured as a contextual inquiry — watch first, ask second. Staff were observed completing real tasks in real time, not narrating hypothetical days. Screen behavior, verbal cues, and physical workarounds were captured as they happened, across three facilities over eight weeks of active fieldwork.

Key Findings

32System touchpoints documented across the intake lifecycle
11Staff-built workarounds operating outside the official process
5Cross-department handoff failure categories with no official owner

Four roles functioned as de facto decision gatekeepers with no formal authority, no system support, and no documented criteria — a structural dynamic that explained chronic delays the system metrics had never attributed to a specific cause.

Deliverables Produced

  • Multi-Actor Service Blueprint — Full intake journey traced across patient, coordinator, back-office reviewer, insurance pre-auth team, and EHR system. Thirty-two touchpoints, eleven workarounds, five handoff failure categories.
  • Persona Set (6) — One primary persona per user group, grounded in triangulated observation and interview evidence. Includes workaround profiles, time pressure patterns, and capability gaps.
  • Process Flow Diagrams — Official process vs. actual observed process, side by side with gap annotations used in executive sessions.
  • Research Repository — Four hundred tagged observation notes and interview transcripts organized by theme, actor, system, and pain point severity in Dovetail.
  • Executive Briefing — Ranked recommendations mapped to operational impact and implementation complexity in plain-language format for non-technical leadership.
  • UX Requirements Document — Fifteen system integration gaps documented as functional requirements, incorporated directly into the technical planning handoff.

Outcomes

🗺
Service Blueprint → Planning ArtifactUsed by IT leadership and the product team to sequence redesign work and define scope — replacing a planning process based on stakeholder assumptions.
♿
WCAG 2.1 Findings → Remediation PlanNine high-severity accessibility failures identified during discovery were prioritized into a remediation plan approved before the redesign began.
🏛
Research Playbook → Internal CapacityDelivered at engagement close so the client's digital transformation office could run follow-on discovery work independently.
Government Grants Equity Research
Nonprofit / State Agency Program — [Redacted] · 2017

Grant Portal Discovery — Equity-Centered Field Research

Ran field research across five community organizations in rural and under-resourced urban areas to observe how applicants with limited administrative capacity engaged with a multi-state grant program. Documented informal reviewer criteria that systematically disadvantaged smaller organizations — findings that shaped portal redesign and rubric revision.

Service Blueprint Journey Map Personas (8) Research Report Accessibility Audit

Background

The program consistently awarded funding to a tier of larger, administratively experienced organizations. Smaller community-based organizations — the intended beneficiaries — applied at low rates, withdrew mid-application, or submitted incomplete materials that automatically disqualified them. No one inside the agency had a clear picture of why.

"The portal had been built based on internal assumptions about applicant capacity that had never been tested against the people actually trying to use it."

Research Methods

Field ObservationContextual Inquiry Participatory Design Workshops Artifact Analysis (63 applications) Stakeholder Interviews (38) Participant Validation

Field sessions documented the physical environment, device limitations, internet access conditions, and social context of the application process — the full situational reality that shaped whether an organization could complete a grant application, not just the interface they used to do it.

Key Findings

14Informal review criteria not in the published scoring rubric
7Critical form comprehension failures found in participatory design sessions
18Formal process steps + 14 informal checkpoints existing only in reviewer judgment

Fourteen undocumented review criteria appeared nowhere in any guidance available to applicants. Organizations with prior grant experience understood them through institutional knowledge. Organizations without that background were evaluated against standards they had no way of knowing existed.

Deliverables Produced

  • Service Blueprint — Six-actor map: state agency, nonprofit staff, community organization applicants, grant reviewers, program director, and federal reporting layer. Eighteen formal steps, fourteen informal checkpoints.
  • Applicant Journey Map — Full application experience with emotion and friction layers, highlighting five points where community-based organizations most commonly disengaged or failed.
  • Persona Set (8) — Spanning organizational type, capacity level, geographic context, and prior grant experience. Used as reference tools for portal redesign and rubric revision.
  • Research Report — Thirty-eight-interview synthesis across four equity-centered themes, each finding supported by field evidence and a specific scoped recommendation.
  • WCAG 2.1 Accessibility Audit — Fourteen barriers preventing assistive technology users from completing the application independently, as a prioritized remediation issue log.
  • Executive Recommendations Brief — Twelve ranked changes for the program director and deputy secretary, framed around program objectives and federal compliance requirements already in scope.

Outcomes

📜
Rubric RevisionThe fourteen undocumented review criteria were incorporated into a revised published scoring rubric, making evaluation standards accessible to all applicants for the first time.
📝
Form Language RewriteSeven comprehension failures drove a plain-language rewrite of the application form completed before the next program cycle opened.
♿
Accessibility RemediationFourteen WCAG 2.1 priority issues were scheduled for the following development sprint as part of the platform remediation plan approved by the agency.
Financial Services Regulatory
Financial Services — [Redacted] · 2019

Dispute Resolution Workflow — Regulatory Process Research

Conducted observational field research across two operations centers, documenting the decision logic agents applied during dispute triage versus the official escalation matrix. Produced a multi-actor service blueprint covering nine manual override steps outside the case management system and four untracked regulatory validation checkpoints.

Service Blueprint Journey Map Personas (3) Section 508 Audit Research Report

Background

The dispute resolution operation had a formal escalation matrix, a trained agent population, and an established case management system. It also had significant handling time variability that no operational metric had attributed to a specific cause, and regulatory checkpoint completions that existed in records but had never been observed in practice.

"The escalation matrix told you what should happen. Forty-two hours of observation told you what did happen — and those were not the same document."

Research Methods

Observational Field Research Contextual Inquiry Post-Observation Interviews Grounded Theory Coding Co-Design Validation Sessions Section 508 Accessibility Review

Forty-two hours of call observation transcripts were coded using grounded theory methodology, producing a sixty-three-code codebook identifying the primary cognitive strategies agents used to compensate for case management system gaps. Those strategies became the foundation for the functional requirements handoff.

Key Findings

9Manual override steps performed outside the case management system on every dispute
4Regulatory validation checkpoints completed verbally with no system record
8Informal routing rules embedded in team culture, absent from training documentation

Four regulatory validation checkpoints appearing as system-logged completions in compliance records were, in practice, completed verbally between agents. The system recorded completion; observation revealed there was no system-supported mechanism for the actual validation — only a convention that the log entry would be made after the fact.

Deliverables Produced

  • Multi-Actor Service Blueprint — Full dispute lifecycle across customer, front-line agent, back-office review team, regulatory reporting unit, and card network interface. Twenty-six touchpoints, nine manual overrides, four untracked regulatory checkpoints.
  • Customer Journey Map — Dispute experience from call to resolution, cross-referenced with the agent-side blueprint to identify eleven moments where customer expectations and internal process timelines diverged.
  • Persona Set (3) — High-volume dispute specialist, escalation reviewer, and compliance analyst — each grounded in observation data with decision triggers and feature requirements.
  • Grounded Theory Codebook — Sixty-three first-order codes and twelve thematic clusters from the full observation dataset, mapped to functional requirements.
  • Section 508 Audit — Seventeen keyboard navigation failures and six screen-reader gaps across the internal case management interface, with a remediation priority matrix.
  • Executive Research Report — Eight research themes, each with observational evidence, a scoped impact statement, and a recommendation framed around regulatory and operational constraints.

Outcomes

📋
Platform Redesign ScopeThe service blueprint and functional requirements defined the scope of the case management platform redesign, replacing a requirements process based on stakeholder assumptions rather than documented behavior.
⚖️
Compliance Process RevisionFour untracked regulatory checkpoints identified in field research were brought to the compliance team's attention. A formal system-supported mechanism for each was incorporated into redesign requirements.
♿
Section 508 RemediationSeventeen keyboard failures and six screen-reader gaps were sequenced into an engineering remediation plan alongside the platform redesign rather than deferred to a separate track.
Discovery Framework

From field to decision.

A structured approach that builds evidence from observation, holds it to scrutiny, and delivers something an organization can actually use.

1

Scoping & Planning

Define research questions, recruit across actor types, design observation and interview protocols for complex multi-stakeholder environments.

2

Field Research

Contextual inquiry and observational sessions on-site. Document workarounds, informal authority, and real process behavior — not idealized flows.

3

Synthesis

Thematic coding, affinity mapping, triangulation. Build service blueprints and process flows from raw evidence, not assumption.

4

Validation

Share findings drafts with participants before delivery. Correct what does not match lived experience. This is how research earns credibility with frontline staff and executives alike.

5

Delivery

Research reports, persona sets, UX requirements, and executive briefings structured around real decisions — not research methodology.
